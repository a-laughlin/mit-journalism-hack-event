Engineering “Addiction”

All your dates are belong to us

Even ethical game designers can be a little scary.

“When a system of rules, resources, interactions and goals are well-designed, gameplay is like mental catnip to us,” Richard Lemarchand told his audience on March 7, 2102, at the international Game Developers Conference in San Francisco. “… Addictive games pull your attention back to them over and over again, and then hold it for a long time. Games that cause people addiction-related problems in their lives are perhaps too good at getting and holding that particular person’s attention.”

Since the inception of the first, simple games, designers have been taking cues from behavioral neuroscience. The literature about game design includes phrases like “time pressure mechanics” that heighten excitement in games for children. Game designer forums break down Flappy Bird and Candy

Crush to inspect the elements that make them unstoppably addictive.

But neuroscientists – particularly addiction researchers – have begun to look back.

The Dope

Addiction researchers are still fine-tuning their ideas about what addiction is and is not.

Mostly they rely on surveys of behavior – are you someone who smokes an occasional joint at a party, or do you have a road map of track marks on your arms? – and neural imaging.

And, when it comes to things that scientists like David Epstein can measure on a PET scan, dopamine is a star.

Dopamine gets a good rap, maybe because it walks just in front of good feelings: Reward. Pleasure. Success.

But dopamine doesn’t make you happy.

“It says to the rest of the brain: ‘Hey! Pay attention to THAT THING! Last time you encountered it, something interesting happened,’” said Epstein, a behavioral neuroscientist at the National Institute on Drug Abuse. “You can see how such a signal might quickly result in pleasure, but also might not.

“One of the major theories of addiction is that the brain has separate systems for ‘wanting’ and ‘liking’ – and dopamine is only behind the ‘wanting,’” Epstein said.

Or as neuroscience blogger Bethany Brookshire described it in Slate, “Many people like to describe a spike in dopamine as ‘motivation’ or ‘pleasure.’ But that’s not quite it. Really, dopamine is signaling feedback for predicted rewards. If you, say, have learned to associate a cue (like a crack pipe) with a hit of crack, you will start getting increases in dopamine in the nucleus accumbens in response to the sight of the pipe, as your brain predicts the reward.”

But your brain also likes surprises.

When the brain spots a pattern that leads to reward, it lets out a little gush of the good dope – but it releases even more dopamine when the reward is unexpected.

Your Brain on Games

This is the idea behind the simple slot machine: Create a game of pure, random chance, but cover it with the kinds of cues that our brain uses to make patterns. Colors. Shapes. Numbers. Give rewards or unpredictable value at irregular intervals.
Let your brain do the rest. (For more on that, check out RadioLab’s “Seeking Patterns” podcast from 2009)

RadioLab’s “Seeking Patterns”

The same principles apply in games that aren’t purely random, where the aim is to solving increasingly difficult puzzles or learning new skills.

In 1998, researchers at Imperial College School of Medicine in London conducted an experiment to measure the amount of dopamine that was released during video game play.

They found that their subjects’ brains reacted to a tank-driving video game by releasing an amount of dopamine comparable to a hit of amphetamines. (For those who haven’t tried speed, imagine a feeling of sharpened anticipation about twice as powerful as listening to a new and beautiful variation of your favorite song.)

Love and Warcraft

The anecdotes of game addiction are now plentiful (and disturbing) enough that even game designers are concerned.

A game called Piano Tiles throws up a warning box when it detects that a player has been using the game for too long – although, without data, how do we know what “too long” might be?

The BBC and others have created news programs around the question of video game addiction – but, again, there’s insufficient data to support definite conclusions.

There are plenty of anecdotes that claim a link between gaming, depression and relationship difficulties – some of it offered by gamers, themselves.

In a LiveJournal devoted to “gamer widows” (and, rarely, widowers), a woman named Jessica wrote, “[H]e’s not going to play video games while he is on the phone with me because I told him that I have to repeat myself a lot [sic] and he said that is what his past fiancee used to do and she hated it too. So I am happy to report that my problem is solved…for now at least =)”

Others are less happy to compromise.

Sixty-five percent of Japanese women said that they would consider it time to divorce a husband “when he spends all day playing computer games.”

And, horribly, there are accounts of real-world deaths linked (sometimes tenuously, sometimes very tenuously) to gaming, including that of an infant whose gamer parents killed her through neglect, which makes this anecdote, from Michael Shermer’s “The Mind of the Market” seem all too possible for humans:

“In 1954, James Olds and Peter Milner accidentally implanted an electrode into the [pleasure centers] of a rat and discovered that it became very energized, so they purposely set up an apparatus such that whenever a rat pressed a bar it generated a small electrical stimulation to the area. The rats pressed the bar until they collapsed, even to the point of forgoing food and water.”

So … now what? Video gaming may look, in some ways, like an addiction. The industry might be shifting closer to something imagined by David Foster Wallace or Tad Williams. But what are the real-world effects?

We don’t know, and probably should.
